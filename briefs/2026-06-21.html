HN: Building reliable agentic AI systems

Published: June 20 at 9:28 PM PDT | Age: 10.1h | Latest visible activity: 7:13 AM PDT | reply now Summary: a Martin Fowler / Bayer write-up on making agentic AI reliable is getting active discussion around evals, workflow boundaries, and operational trust.

Why Robby has standing: this is exactly the bridge between AI demo magic and durable web/professional workflows that agencies and WordPress product teams have to cross.

Best angle: "reliability" is not only model quality; it is approvals, reversible actions, plain-language logs, and handoff points that non-AI teammates can understand.

Draft: The part I keep coming back to is that reliability for agentic web work is as much product design as model behavior. In client or team workflows, you need the agent to do useful work, but you also need approvals that map to how humans think, a clear audit trail, and easy rollback when the agent was technically successful but wrong for the business. That layer is what turns "it worked in a demo" into something an agency or product team can trust.

Risk note: keep it experience-backed and avoid making it sound like a product tease.

HN: Anthropic to Require ID Verification for Certain Capabilities Starting July 8

Published: June 21 at 5:44 AM PDT | Age: 1.8h | Latest visible activity: 7:32 AM PDT | reply now Summary: a fast-moving thread on Anthropic's ID-verification requirement for some capabilities is turning into a broader debate about AI access, abuse controls, privacy, and platform trust.

Why Robby has standing: Robby can speak from the operator side of giving AI systems meaningful power while still needing customer trust, guardrails, and understandable permissions.

Best angle: identity and capability gating are blunt tools; the more useful product layer is a transparent trust model that explains what gets unlocked, why, and how the user stays in control.

Draft: For AI tools that can affect real systems, I understand why vendors reach for identity/capability gates. The product problem is making the trust model legible. What exactly gets unlocked, why does it need a stronger gate, what data is retained, and what control does the user keep? If those answers are vague, even a reasonable abuse-prevention step feels like a power grab.

Risk note: privacy/politics thread; stay on product trust and permissions.
