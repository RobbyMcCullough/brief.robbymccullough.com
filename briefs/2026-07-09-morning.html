Summary: A developer is describing coding-agent use as an attention, ownership, and dependency problem rather than just a productivity question.

Why Robby has standing: This maps directly to client-site and product-team workflows where AI can create speed, but only if the human still owns the system and review path.

Best angle: Agents work best as bounded collaborators inside a system of architecture, diffs, tests, review, and rollback; if they replace understanding, the workflow is upside down.

Draft: "I do not think this is only a skill issue. The workflow changes the job. The trap is treating the agent as the owner of the work and yourself as the person waiting for output. The healthier version, at least in web/product work, is more like: keep the architecture small enough to hold in your head, give the agent narrow tasks, review diffs as if a junior teammate wrote them, and keep tests/rollback boring. The leverage is real, but the ownership still has to stay with the operator."

Risk: The original post has an emotional tone; avoid sounding like a productivity guru or minimizing the frustration.
